Out of sync

Photography has never truly been objective. Even before digital editing, images were already manipulated, staged, retouched, and reconstructed. The camera never simply captured reality; it shaped it. What we call “truth” in photography has always been unstable.

Today, however, images no longer try to hide manipulation through collage or visible intervention. Instead, they aim for perfection. Faces are filtered, bodies sharpened, skin erased into smooth surfaces. The edited image now presents itself as natural. The more “real” an image appears online, the more artificial it often becomes.

SYSTEM/SKIN responds to this hyper-polished visual culture through sabotage. Glitches, low resolution, visual noise, distortion, and fragmentation interrupt the illusion of perfection. They expose the image as fragile and constructed rather than fixed and flawless.

Within the platform, bodies transform into alien or creature-like forms, moving away from recognizable human identity. These mutations become a way of escaping the pressure to always appear coherent, visible, and consumable. The distorted body refuses immediate readability.

In a world obsessed with high definition, blurred and damaged images can feel strangely more honest. Older technologies, corrupted files, and imperfect textures leave room for ambiguity, memory, and imagination. They remind us that reality itself is unstable.
